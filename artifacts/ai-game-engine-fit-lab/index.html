Interactive engine-choice lab · AI-heavy games

Pick the engine by the AI workload, not by the logo.

Godot, Unity, Unreal, and platform/custom engines can all power “AI games.” The useful question is narrower: what job is the AI doing, where must it run, and how much production machinery does the team need?

Core insight: “AI game” is not one category. A 2D cozy game with an LLM storyteller, a neural-net mobile mechanic, a thousand-agent city, and a cinematic stealth shooter want different engines.

Decision grammar

Five questions beat engine tribalism.

If you can answer these, the engine shortlist usually becomes obvious.

1. Runtime or tooltime?

AI in the shipped game has different needs from AI used to make art, levels, dialogue drafts, or tests.

2. On-device or server?

On-device inference values deployment/runtime support. Server LLMs care more about networking, latency, moderation, and cost controls.

3. 2D, stylized, or cinematic 3D?

Visual target changes the production burden more than the AI label does.

4. How many agents?

One companion NPC is design-heavy. Thousands of agents become simulation architecture and tooling.

5. Who is building it?

Solo/open-source teams, C# studios, AAA teams, and UGC creators optimize for different risks.
